It's been a bad year for internet privacy in the United States. 2018 saw some of the world's biggest companies experience catastrophic data breaches. In one of the largest cyber hacks in history, MyFitnessPal, owned by Under Armour, disclosed that 150 million of its users had their emails, usernames, and hashed passwords hacked. Facebook has had a year, alright. We all know about the 50+ million users that were harvested through the Cambridge Analytica data scandal. Yet, Facebook managed to outdo themselves this past September when the social media giant revealed that 90 million users had been compromised again by hackers. Aetna, a national healthcare provider, compromised the privacy of 12,000 of its HIV-positive customers and agreed to a reported $17 million dollar settlement last January.

There are dozens of more examples from this year alone. What concerns me the most is that some of the biggest names in their industries — Under Armour, Facebook, and Aetna — were not prepared to protect their users' sensitive data and information from hackers. If the biggest, most well-funded companies in the world can't protect our data, what does that say about the state of cybersecurity? The fact of the matter is that no company, regardless of its size, can protect your information online from being compromised. It is up to us, as individuals, to fortify our accounts and protect ourselves online.

I have known about the risk of hackers and invasion of my online privacy for months, even years, yet I never felt inspired to do anything about it. With the proliferation of cyber hacks occurring all over the web, I finally took the plunge and decided to protect myself in the following ways:

## 1. Started using LastPass

[LastPass](https://www.lastpass.com) is one of the many available password managers out there. The free\* service encrypts all of your passwords in a secure vault which you can access via a mobile app, browser extension, or website. LastPass will even generate automatic secure passwords for you when you sign up for new services or when you reset your password for existing accounts. The best part about LastPass is that you never have to remember your passwords again. The service will autofill your username and password to any account across devices.

## 2. Got off Facebook and its subsidiaries

I deleted both of the Menlo Park-based firm's social media apps off my phone and deactivated\*\* both of my accounts. Facebook has shown time and time again that it does not care about your privacy and will shamelessly sell your data for profit. Facebook's services are also one of the most direct ways that a would-be hacker can find you online. You have your name, email, phone number, and increasingly your billing information linked to your profile, making your Facebook and Instagram accounts treasure chests for would-be digital assailants.

A fringe benefit of deleting these apps is that you retrieve up to two hours, the daily average time that Instagram and Facebook users spent in app last year, per day that you can now spend on achieving your goals rather than watching someone else achieve their own. How do you think I found the time to write this article?

## 3. Deleted or fortified old accounts

Deleting or disabling old accounts that you no longer use will limit your exposure to being compromised online. Your email account from high school probably still has a phone number, former address, or contact that can link you to your current and more valuable online accounts. Still want that old Twitter handle from high school? That's fine, but make sure that you change the password to something modern and hard to guess. 'Iliketurtles3' isn't going to cut it as a password in 2019. Taking the time to delete or fortify these accounts might be tedious, but think about how strong your digital account fortress will be when you're done.

## 4. Started using Brave Browser

[Brave Browser](https://brave.com), built on Chromium, is a secure, fast, and private web browser. Brave automatically blocks all advertisements and most pop-ups, creating a browsing experience up to 8x faster than Chrome or Safari on mobile, and up to 2x faster than Chrome on desktop. This savings in time also translates to actual dollar savings. The ads that Brave blocks on mobile never get downloaded to your phone, dramatically reducing your phone's data usage. Less data used = less data needed. Brave gives you the freedom to see the same amount of content on your phone with a less expensive data plan. Most importantly, Brave blocks all unwanted content and trackers by default, as many as 70 per site, protecting your browsing activity, location, and other browsing activity from being exploited.

## 5. Started using DuckDuckGo Extension

[DuckDuckGo](https://duckduckgo.com) browser extensions (available for Chrome, Firefox, and Brave) will force websites to encrypt your access to a given website, protecting your data from prying eyes. It will also block tracker networks that benefit from taking your data and sending it to various advertising and data collection agencies all over the web. Last, DuckDuckGo assigns each website you visit a letter grade based on the level of tracking and privacy standards that the site engages in. This way you can definitively know how your favorite websites rate in the privacy department.

## 6. Switched to Apple

This year I made the jump from Google's Android platform to Apple's iOS, purchasing an iPhone and iPad. One of the main reasons that I wanted to make the jump is that Apple takes privacy so seriously. You might not realize it, but all iMessages and iOS backups are encrypted and protected from outside users, meaning you'll never have your information/communication exploited or shared without your permission no matter who asks.

## 7. Using 2-factor authentication (2FA) for any websites that enable it

Say someone does get to your email, password, cell phone number, and other personal account information. It happens. Maybe you left your computer at a Starbucks with your accounts open, or you were a part of one of the dozens of data breaches cited earlier. With 2FA, you are protected from unnecessary logins because any time someone logs into your account you are prompted to enter a 4–6 digit PIN number that is available only on your phone's 2FA app. I suggest using Symantec's VIP Access app, available on the App Store and Google Play.

In today's constantly-connected world, protecting yourself online has never been more important. Identity theft can strike any of us and wreak havoc on our credit scores, bank accounts, relationships, or status in society. These steps are just the beginning of my journey to fully protect myself when using the internet. I will continue to look for new ways to protect myself and my information moving forward.

How do you protect yourself online? Stay safe out there!

*\*LastPass also has a premium subscription that allows family password sharing, 1GB of encrypted storage, and a few other perks.*

*\*\*I didn't delete my Facebook or Instagram account, and opted to deactivate instead, since there is a chance that Facebook, at some time in the future, will change its ways and start taking privacy more seriously. When or if that day comes, I would consider using the service again and I'd like to not have to add all my friends/groups again.*
