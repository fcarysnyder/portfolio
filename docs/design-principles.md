## Durability

*To develop.*

A design has to hold up as the product evolves. The features that get added next quarter, the edge cases that surface once real users touch it, the integrations nobody anticipated at concept time. Durability is the question of whether the structure underneath will absorb those pressures or have to be rebuilt to accommodate them.

## Stance, not fiat

A designer comes back with a stance. Not a deck. Not a memo. A proposal: how about this, and here's why. It's not dictatorial. It's not fiat. It's a direction concrete enough that the team can stress-test it, refine it, sometimes reject it. The momentum comes from having something to push against, together.

*Developed in: [Clarity is the Deliverable](/writing/clarity-is-the-deliverable).*

## Fidelity matched to the question

Low fidelity is for problem-seeking. High fidelity is for problem-solving. The order matters, because once you invest in visual polish, the sunk cost makes it psychologically harder to change the structure underneath. Crude fidelity is an epistemological tool, not an aesthetic limitation.

*Developed in: [The Bad Ideas Look Shippable](/writing/the-bad-ideas-look-shippable).*

## Feasibility as creative parameter

Design grounded in feasibility - design that treats engineering constraints as creative parameters, not obstacles - is what separates a concept from a plan. When you skip that grounding, you hand a team a vision they may not be able to execute.

## Reduction over generation

Design is the act of collapsing an overwhelming field of possibilities into a single coherent direction. Every decision is a small version of the same move: not that, this. The artifact is the record of those decisions made visible; the value is in the reduction.

*Developed in: [Clarity is the Deliverable](/writing/clarity-is-the-deliverable).*

## Form follows function

*To develop.*

Aesthetics and visual consistency matter. They just come second-order to good design.

Dieter Rams' work and the NASA brand are the references I keep coming back to. Both come from designers who focused almost entirely on function, and the form followed because the function was distilled into what the product or device needs to do in its circumstance.

Embellishing the ISS with visual styles can only be afforded so long as the humans inside don't perish. That's the hierarchy made literal. Function is what keeps people alive. Form is what's added once function is sound.

The order matters. Aesthetics aren't separate from design; they're earned by the clarity of the decisions underneath.
