A product manager on my team presented a dashboard to a client last week. The dashboard shows synthetic readings for energy usage, rates, and equipment efficiency for large, industrial cold storage facilities. The data visualizations cover standard time ranges of the last 1, 7, 30, and 90 days. The client looked at it, nodded, and said: "This is great. Can we get week over week and month over month comparisons too?"

My colleague messaged me on Slack right after. I told him that's the type of feedback we love to hear. "This is good, but you know what would be great..." - that sentence means the client already sees the value. They're not questioning whether the dashboard should exist; they're imagining how they'd use it every morning. That's not criticism. That's validation in disguise.

## "Yes, and" vs "Yes, but"

There's a distinction every designer and PM needs to internalize: the difference between feedback that builds on your work and feedback that questions whether the work should exist. These sound similar. They mean very different things.

Here's what "yes, and" feedback sounds like in practice: "Can we add filtering by region?" or "What if this updated in real time?" or "I'd love to see this broken down by team." The user has accepted your framework. They're living inside the product mentally, thinking about their workflow, their morning routine, their team's needs. They skipped past "do I want this?" and landed on "how do I want *more* of this?"

Compare that to critical feedback: "I don't understand what this is showing me." "Where do I find the thing I actually need?" "This doesn't work for my use case." That signals something different:  misalignment, not enhancement. It tells you the foundation needs attention. The user isn't building on your framework; they're questioning whether the framework fits.

Both types of feedback are useful. They just mean very different things.

## Not all "yes, and" is real

Here's where this gets tricky: not every "can we also..." is validation. If you can't tell the difference, the framework falls apart.

#### Polite deflection

Some stakeholders say "this is great, but can we add X?" when they actually mean "this doesn't solve my problem and I don't want to be rude." The enhancement request is a face-saving exit. You see this a lot with internal senior stakeholders who don't want to crush someone in a meeting.  The tells are tone of voice and body language - short responses, no follow-up questions, a quick pivot to the next agenda item. Sometimes you have both signals and sometimes you're reading a Slack message and all you have is a vibe. A flat "sure, could we also add X" with no enthusiasm, no specifics, no follow-up. When in doubt, ask to get on a call. You'll know within thirty seconds.

#### Scatter without specificity

Sometimes users pile on feature requests because the core thing isn't clicking, but they can't articulate why. "Add filtering, add export, add real-time updates" - when the requests come in a scatter pattern with no thread connecting them, the user is grasping, not building. The tell is always specificity and energy. "Can we get week over week comparisons?" is grounded in a real workflow. That person already knows when they'd check that number and what they'd do with it. "Can we add more charts?" is vague. It's filler, not vision.

The distinguishing factor is specificity. Real "yes, and" feedback comes from someone who has mentally placed your product into their day. They can tell you *when* they'd use it, *what* they'd compare, *who* on their team needs it. Polite noise stays abstract.

## Learning to hear it

Real "yes, and" feedback often *feels* like criticism when you first hear it. "Can we get week over week?" registers as "what you built isn't enough." Your instinct is to defend the scope, explain the constraints, justify the decisions you made. But the user isn't pushing back. They're pulling forward. The feature request is proof of concept.

We who build software - designers, engineers, and PMs - don't know what people want or how they'll use what we build until they tell us. We have our best guesses, but that's all they are. When real feedback arrives, it won't sound like "great job, 100%." It sounds like "can we also get..."

Hear "and." Add it to the backlog. You don't need to drop everything and build week over week comparisons on the spot. Validation rarely announces itself. Learning to parse it is the skill.
