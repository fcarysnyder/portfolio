My aunt asked me recently if her kids should still bother learning to code. "Isn't AI going to replace all of that?" she said, with the quiet concern of someone who's been reading too many headlines.

My answer was yes and no — which is, I realize, the most unsatisfying thing you can say to someone looking for a straight answer. But it's the honest one. Because what's happening right now isn't the end of our work. It's the end of slow work. And if you're a designer or developer, that distinction matters more than you might think.

## The skill has changed. The knowledge hasn't.

Let's start with the uncomfortable part: the raw skill of writing code has plummeted in value. I don't mean it's worthless. I mean that the gap between someone who can write a React component from memory and someone who can describe what they want to an AI tool has narrowed dramatically. The syntax is increasingly commoditized.

But here's what hasn't changed — the architectural thinking. Knowing *what* to build, *why* to build it, and *how* it should fit into the larger system. That knowledge is more valuable now than it's ever been, precisely because the bottleneck has shifted. When production speed is no longer the constraint, the constraint becomes judgment.

If you're a designer, think about it this way: AI can generate fifty layout variations in the time it takes you to sketch one. But it can't tell you which one actually solves the user's problem. It can't weigh the business context against the technical constraints against the emotional experience you're trying to create. That calculus is still yours.

If you're a developer, the parallel is just as clear. AI can scaffold an entire application in minutes. But it doesn't know your team's conventions, your infrastructure's quirks, or the three subtle reasons the last team tried this approach and abandoned it. Context is the new competitive advantage, and context lives in people.

## Velocity is the new metric

Here's the paradox that I think most people miss: AI doesn't reduce the need for engineers and designers. It increases it.

When you can move faster, you *do* move faster. And when you move faster, you need more people to orchestrate, review, and refine the output. The work doesn't disappear — it compresses and multiplies. A team that once shipped a feature per sprint can now ship three, but each one still needs someone with taste and judgment to make sure it's right.

This is already playing out. The companies that have leaned hardest into AI tooling aren't laying off their design and engineering teams. They're asking them to do more, to cover more surface area, to operate at a higher altitude. The job description hasn't shrunk. It's shifted upward.

For designers, this means less time pushing pixels and more time defining systems, setting constraints, and making the judgment calls that shape entire product experiences. For developers, it means less time writing boilerplate and more time on architecture, code review, and the gnarly integration problems that AI still can't untangle on its own.

The velocity increase is real. But velocity without direction is just chaos. That's where we come in.

## The "why" is still ours

Business is ultimately about people. Every product decision, every design choice, every line of code exists in service of some human need or desire. And the further AI advances, the more that fundamental truth comes into focus.

AI can handle the syntax. It can handle the volume. It can generate, iterate, and optimize at a scale that would have seemed absurd five years ago. But it can't handle the *why*. It doesn't understand why a particular user flow feels frustrating, only that engagement metrics dipped. It doesn't know why your team chose to prioritize accessibility over a flashy animation, only that the spec says so. It can't sit in a room with a stakeholder who's nervous about a launch and read between the lines of what they're actually asking for.

The danger isn't that AI will replace the people who do this kind of work. The danger is that some people will convince themselves they don't need to do it anymore — that the tools are good enough to skip the thinking. They're not. And the gap between teams that understand this and teams that don't is going to widen fast.

## What this means for us

If you're early in your career, learn the tools. Get fast with them. But invest even more in the things AI can't replicate: systems thinking, communication, the ability to hold a complex problem in your head and reason about tradeoffs. Those skills were always important. Now they're the whole game.

If you're mid-career or senior, your experience just became your superpower. The pattern recognition you've built over years of shipping products, the intuition for what will and won't work — that's the layer AI can't touch. Lean into it. Teach it. The people around you need it more than ever.

The end of slow work isn't something to fear. It's something to prepare for. And if you're reading this, you're already doing that.
