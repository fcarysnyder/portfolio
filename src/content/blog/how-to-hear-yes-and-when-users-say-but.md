# How to Hear "Yes, And" When Users Say "But"

A product manager on my team presented a dashboard to a client this week. The dashboard showed synthetic readings for energy usage, rates, and equipment efficiency for large, industrial cold storage facilities. The data visualizations cover standard time ranges of the last 1, 7, 30, and 90 days. The client looked at it, nodded, and said: "This is great. Can we get week over week and month over month comparisons too?"

My colleague messaged me on Slack right after. I told him that's the type of feedback we love to hear. "This is good, but you know what would be great..." - that sentence means the client already sees the value. They already want it. They're not questioning whether the dashboard should exist; they're imagining how they'd use it every morning. That's not criticism. That's validation in disguise.

## "Yes, and" vs "Yes, but"

There's a distinction every designer and PM needs to internalize: the difference between feedback that builds on your work and feedback that questions whether the work should exist. These sound similar. They mean very different things.

Here's what "yes, and" feedback sounds like in practice: "Can we add filtering by region?" or "What if this updated in real time?" or "I'd love to see this broken down by team." The user has accepted your framework. They're living inside the product mentally, thinking about their workflow, their morning routine, their team's needs. They skipped past "do I want this?" and landed on "how do I want *more* of this?"

Compare that to critical feedback: "I don't understand what this is showing me." "Where do I find the thing I actually need?" "This doesn't work for my use case." That signals something different - misalignment, not enhancement. It tells you the foundation needs attention. The user isn't building on your framework; they're questioning whether the framework fits.

Both types of feedback are useful. They just mean very different things.

## Not all "yes, and" is real

Here's where this gets tricky. Not every "can we also..." is genuine validation. If you can't tell the difference, the framework falls apart.

**Polite deflection.** Some stakeholders say "this is great, but can we add X?" when they actually mean "this doesn't solve my problem and I don't want to be rude." The enhancement request is a face-saving exit. You see this a lot with senior stakeholders who don't want to crush someone in a meeting. The feedback sounds constructive, but the body language tells a different story - short responses, no follow-up questions, a quick pivot to the next agenda item.

**Scope creep as avoidance.** Users sometimes pile on feature requests because the core thing isn't clicking, but they can't articulate why. "Add filtering, add export, add real-time updates" can mean "I don't know what I'd actually do with this." When the requests come in a scatter pattern with no thread connecting them, the user is grasping. They're not building on your framework; they're looking for a framework that works.

**The enthusiasm gap.** There's a difference between a user who leans forward and starts sketching workflows on a whiteboard vs. one who nods and says "yeah, could we also get..." The signal is in the specificity and the energy, not just the words. "Can we get week over week comparisons?" is grounded in a real workflow - that person already knows when they'd check that number and what they'd do with it. "Can we add more charts?" is vague. It's filler, not vision.

The distinguishing factor is always specificity. Real "yes, and" feedback comes from someone who has mentally placed your product into their day. They can tell you *when* they'd use it, *what* they'd compare, *who* on their team needs it. Polite noise stays abstract.

## Validation doesn't sound like praise

The tricky part is that real "yes, and" feedback often *feels* like criticism when you first hear it. "Can we get week over week?" registers as "what you built isn't enough." Your instinct is to defend the scope, explain the constraints, justify the decisions you made. When we hear disagreement, we get defensive. When we get defensive, we stop listening to what the feedback actually means.

But the real issue here isn't disagreement. The user isn't pushing back. They're pulling forward. They've accepted what you presented and they're ideating *within* your framework - which is high signal that the design is working. The feature request is proof of concept.

This is especially true when the feedback comes from external users, the people who will actually live with the product. We as people who build software - designers, engineers, and PMs - don't know what people want or how they'll use what we build. We have guesses. When actual users start telling us what they want next, within the structure we gave them, that's the closest thing to real validation we get.

## What to do when you hear it

Recognize it. Then add it to the backlog.

You don't need to drop everything and build week over week comparisons on the spot. The feedback itself confirms that your product is valuable to the customer. The MVP landed. It fits a need. You can always layer on more value over time, and now you have a prioritized list of enhancements that came directly from the people who use the product - not from internal assumptions about what users might want.

The next time a stakeholder looks at your work and says "this is great, but can we also..." - don't hear "but." Hear "and." You already did the hard part - you built something people want. Now learn to hear it. Validation rarely announces itself. It shows up as a feature request, a casual "what if," a question that assumes your product is already part of someone's morning.
