"AI makes it too easy to sprint in the wrong direction."

An engineer I work with said this recently, and I haven't been able to stop thinking about it. Right now, the dominant narrative is that AI is replacing everything. Design is dead. Frontend development is dead. The people getting the most attention are the ones pushing AI the furthest, the fastest, and showing the receipts on LinkedIn. Questioning that narrative feels like admitting you can't keep up.

But there's a difference between pushing boundaries and ignoring risks. We talk about AI velocity constantly - how fast you can ship, how many iterations you can run, how quickly a prototype goes from idea to working code. [Companies are even creating tokenmaxxing leaderboards now](https://www.theinformation.com/articles/meta-employees-vie-ai-token-legend-status). Velocity *is* the metric. But velocity without direction is just expensive movement, and the tools we have today make expensive movement trivially easy if we aren't careful. Nowhere is this more visible than in prototyping, where the line between pushing boundaries and ignoring risks has gotten dangerously thin.

## The governor is gone

Prototypes used to have a natural cost. If you wanted to test an idea before AI, you had options, and none of them were free. You could sketch it on paper. You could learn Figma and build a clickable prototype. You could write actual code, which meant days or weeks of work before you had anything to show. That cost was a governor. It forced you to be intentional about *what* you were testing and *why*, because you couldn't afford to test everything.

But the cost did something else, too. It forced you to think about what you were building while you were building it. Every line you wrote, every screen you laid out, every interaction you wired up was a moment where you had to confront your own assumptions. "Does this make sense? Is this the right flow? Am I solving the right problem?" The friction was a thinking process. Designers call this design thinking: the discipline of diverging into possibilities before converging on a solution. You don't have to be a designer to practice this; you just have to be willing to sit with uncertainty long enough to learn something.

If you outsource design thinking to an AI, you remove the governor. You can describe a general idea and have it make a hundred thousand decisions on your behalf in an afternoon. You can get a working application, backend, APIs, real data, and enough polish to demo to stakeholders. The cost of building a prototype has dropped to nearly zero. The cost of building the *wrong* thing has not. And the thinking process that used to be embedded in the building? You can't see it in the artifact. A polished prototype built on deep research and a polished prototype built from AI defaults look identical from the outside. Both feel like evidence of good decisions. Sometimes they are. Sometimes they're just defaults the AI chose on your behalf.

Low-fidelity has always let designers go wide. Sketches and Figma frames are cheap, throwaway, and easy to share. Code-prototyping is a different medium. It takes more time than you'd think, it costs tokens, and it's harder to multiplayer. Going wide is what the early design phase is for. Code isn't always the right way to do it.

When building is cheap, people build more. More output means more noise, and the ratio of good ideas to bad ones doesn't improve just because the volume went up. The problem is that AI makes it difficult to tell signal from noise, because noise now has commit history, a URL, a color scheme, and real data. Too often, bad ideas become indistinguishable from good ones, and when velocity is the metric, nobody slows down long enough to figure out which is which.

## Maximum viable product

There's a reason the industry landed on "minimum viable product" as a concept. You build the smallest thing that isolates a single assumption. The rest of the product holds steady, the assumption is the variable, and the test tells you something you can act on. That's the scientific method, scoped down to a feature. The emphasis is on viable, not impressive, and on minimum, not everything you can think of. You keep each bet small enough that you can afford to be wrong, because you're only wrong about one thing at a time.

AI inverts this. When building is nearly free, the instinct is to build everything. Why ship a simple prototype to test one assumption when you can ship a full-featured application that covers all of them? Why sketch a wireframe when you can generate production-ready code? The result is what I'd call maximum viable product: something that looks complete, feels complete, and creates a pressure to be right that makes it almost impossible to walk back. The more you've built, the less you can afford to be wrong, so you double down instead of stepping back.

When everything is new at once and everything is polished to the same level, there's no baseline. The flow, the data model, the copy, the visual language, the interactions, all rendered at the same fidelity. Going broad isn't the problem. Design has always done that, sometimes with sketches, sometimes with walls of Figma frames. The problem is polish applied evenly across all of it, because polish makes every choice look intentional whether it was or not. 

The pressure to be right is the trap. Once you've invested time, energy, and money in something, your psychology works against you. We are wired to protect our investments. The more we put into something, the harder it becomes to walk away from it, even when walking away is the right call. This is the [sunk cost fallacy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thinking,_Fast_and_Slow), one of the most common logical errors in decision-making. Too often, the question shifts from "is this the right thing to build?" to "how do we ship what we've already built?"

A maximum viable product looks especially attractive to businesses chasing quick wins. "If we can build something this polished this fast, why not skip straight to market?" The speed feels like a competitive advantage. But speed without thought is just a faster way to build something nobody asked for. There's no shortcut to making better decisions, and the sunk cost fallacy makes it harder to admit that once you've committed.

## Could vs. should

Ian Malcolm has a line in Jurassic Park that I quote often: "Your scientists were so preoccupied with whether or not they could, they didn't stop to think if they should."

Jurassic Park is a parable about deploying powerful technology without being honest about what you don't understand. The product was amazing. The technology worked. The grounds were beautiful. The gift shop was stocked. Still, the whole thing collapsed. Hammond had designed for success and nothing else. The park didn't fail because the science was bad, it failed because confidence outpaced understanding.

The same trap waits in AI tooling. The question "should we build this, for whom, and why" is easily skipped because building something impressive feels productive and convincing. It's hard to ask "should this exist" when it already does.

## Curiosity as discipline

The antidote is remembering what prototypes are *for*, and that's the thing Hammond got wrong. The park wasn't a prototype. It was a maximum viable product. Hammond skipped straight to T-Rex when the MVP was a smaller predator, with lower risk, to see if containment actually worked.

Prototypes used to have a clear purpose: test an interaction, stress-test a layout, prove a concept, get feedback. The constraint forced clarity about what you were trying to learn. That clarity is still available. It just requires discipline now, because the constraints won't impose it for you.

The best teams I've worked with approach prototypes with curiosity, not conviction. They build something to learn, not to prove they're right. Every prototype names its variable and holds the rest as control. The discipline isn't gatekeeping or sign-off, it's being honest about what the artifact can actually teach you. A prototype that changes one thing can teach you something. A prototype that changes everything can only impress you. That's the trap.

Prototyping isn't always about testing. Sometimes it's about inspiring: pushing what design could be, surfacing possibilities that static frames can't show. Exploration counts as discipline when it's named. Chasing an idea further than you could in Figma, feeling an interaction in code, seeing what a flow does with real data - that's design at its best, and AI extends what designers can do here. The variable doesn't have to be "is this measurably better." It can be "what does this feel like" or "is there something here at all." The control is still the rest of the product. The discipline is still being honest about what you're trying to learn.

## The infinite game

Building products is an [infinite game](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Infinite_Game). You're not solving the customer's problem and moving on. You're committing to a cycle of building, learning, and improving that never really ends. That's what MVP actually is - not a shortcut, but a posture. Maximum viable product assumes you got it right the first time. Hammond assumed he got it right the first time. The infinite game assumes you didn't, and that's the point.

Maximum viable prototypes aren't always wrong. A time-boxed sprint to explore a new direction, scoped as throwaway from day one - that's a legitimate tool. I've done it. The difference is whether thinking was applied. A prototype that captures what you learned, what you tested, and what you'd do differently is evidence of understanding. A prototype that just exists, polished and undocumented, is confidence dressed up as design. The danger is when nobody scopes it as throwaway. The rough thinking behind a polished output gets mistaken for a finished decision, and the prototype quietly becomes the product.

But sometimes an exploration sticks on purpose. It's good enough to use, useful enough to ship. That's the infinite game working, not the trap - the question just shifts from "is this it?" to "what's the next iteration?"

Next time someone hands you a polished prototype, ask what variable it isolates and what it holds still. If everything is new, nothing is a test.
